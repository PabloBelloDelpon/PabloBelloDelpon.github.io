[comment]: <> (Introduction)
A while ago, David Garcia and I started exploring how music consumption had evolved during the short period that streaming platforms have been around. In particular, we wanted to know whether popular music is increasingly globalized as a result of the advent of streaming. 

[comment]: <> (Data and first figure)
To answer this question, we collected all the data we had access to from Spotify and Itunes. Because individual-level data is not made public, we used the charts, so it is important to keep in mind that our results are more about pop-music (or whatever it is most popular in a country) and not so much about music in general. Using the daily two hundred most listened songs on Spotify we looked at how much countries’ charts overlap. We had data for 51 countries and three and a half years. After calculating the number of songs that two countries have in common we found that geographical location seems to be highly correlated with how much music two countries share, with a few notable exceptions. Of these, Spain is the most noticeable one (at least for me and David, who are both from Spain), being closer to the Latin-American cluster than to any other European country.

[comment]: <> (Hypothesis)
What seemed more interested was to look at how these distances between countries were evolving. This question is important because governments, especially European governments, for decades have been protecting “local culture” because they thought that modernity would devour it. This fear stems from the Globalization narrative, common in social theory, even though, at least in regards to the music market, there is evidence pointing in the opposite direction (cite here). More recently, the increasing dominance of streaming has only reinforced this perception, since they have enabled easy access for millions of users to a global market of music. In such a global market, music has very few barriers to spread across countries, thus one would expect to find increasingly the same songs charting across the globe.  

[comment]: <> (Results)
However, what we found is quite the opposite. Instead of becoming more similar, charts are increasingly diverse across countries. This trend is driven by the presence of local music on the charts, which has increased steadily. While in 2017 an average of 85\% of the songs on the charts came from abroad, at the beginning of 2020 it was approximately 10 percentage points less. Still, a broad majority of the songs that have a presence in our charts are foreign, so policymakers might be right in worrying about local culture. We also observed that the diversity of artists and record labels on the charts is growing. This means that there are more opportunities for artists and small labels to make it to the charts.  Lastly, we also found that 'global hits' are increasingly rare. The chances of songs spreading to dozens of countries are becoming smaller.

![alt text](/images/figure_3.png)

[comment]: <> (Discussion)
We argue that digital music, being cheaper to produce has generated a (random) long tail of music production. Artists now do not need expensive equipment or back up from a big label to create and promote their music. Then there is the fact that, under the same conditions, we tend to choose local music instead of international (we understand the lyrics, know the artists, etc). Thus, with a growing offer of local music listeners do not have to resort that much to international music, and some of these local songs eventually make it to the charts, where we observe them.
